A Chat with

Gae Walters

Earlier this year, Gae led six in-office Myers Briggs Type Indicator (MBTI) sessions, along with three webinars. We caught up with Gae prior to next week's leadership-focused MBTI deep dive with Virtuoso's NLL and S-Team for a brief chat.

It’s been three months since you led 209 Virtuoso employees through the Myers Briggs Type Indicator (MBTI). What’s the most important point you’d want to reinforce with them?

Gae: The MBTI shows us the different ways people see and understand the world. The most important point is that a team, an organization, in fact, the world needs all eight approaches to be successful and all eight need to be respected for the unique value each brings to the table.

As Virtuoso opens offices in new corners of the world, are there any MBTI-based words of wisdom you can offer us as we evolve from our US-headquartered roots to a multicultural global organization?

When I facilitate Myers Briggs workshops around the world, I find that we humans are more alike than we are different. Under the surface veneer of our cultures, we share many of the same psychological needs. For example, in order to learn new processes, solve problems and become energized, people who prefer Introversion typically need silence, solitude, and time for reflection — regardless of their country of origin. Whereas someone who prefers Extraversion typically needs interaction, dialogue, and discussion — again, regardless of their country of origin. The MBTI, therefore, is a great tool for building bridges of understanding across cultures and countries.

So, to call back to how you opened our workshops, some of the behavioral differences people may attribute to culture, might actually be rooted in MBTI?

Possibly. People are a complex combination of environmental influences, cultural influences, philosophical beliefs, familial teachings, and personality preferences. My suggestion would be to get to know people as individuals and not to stereotype them based on culture or country or MBTI letters!
